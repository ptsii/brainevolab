A Discourse Analytic Study of Language Attitudes amongst the Speakers of Burushaski

Samina KHAN

The present study explores the language attitudes amongst a community in a small town called Hunza in Northern Pakistan. It is a part of the autonomous region formerly called Northern Areas but later named as Gilgit-Baltistan. It investigates the attitudes of the Burusho people from Hunza towards the Hunza dialect of Burushaski, Urdu and English.The community speaks an isolated language known as Burushaski, which has no known relation to any other language spoken in this region. Although the community is relatively small, multiethnic and multilingual communities surround it, which makes the case an interesting one to investigate. The speakers of Burushaski grow up speaking it as their native language but also spend time learning Urdu at school, which has the status of the national language, as well as learning English, which is considered the official spoken language.

The fact that this region is geographically isolated from the rest of Pakistan by mountain ranges contributes to the uniqueness of its linguistic diversity and when the individuals move out of Hunza in search of work and education, it gives rise to attitudes that are interesting to be explored. The eldest speakers of language live in Hunza, while the younger generation has been moving to different parts of the country for pursuits in education and job opportunities. Thus, speakers of Burushaski are in constant language contact with speakers of Urdu or English, mostly first in academic and then work settings. .

The focus of my research is the community that speaks Hunza dialect, which is spoken by the Burusho people of Hunza. The Burusho people of Hunza, although speak one language, are divided into different caste systems. These caste systems are not based on socioeconomic status since most of the Burusho people belong to the middle class and lower middle class and the younger generation is mostly educated. It is a community with strong family ties and working in diverse fields.  The younger generation has moved out of the region in search of jobs and studies. A majority of them reside in Karachi and Rawalpindi, two major cities of Pakistan. The group that I interviewed was mainly from Rawalpindi. Most of them were students who were either working, continuing a higher degree, or had just graduated. They had spent most of their life in either Gilgit or some other part of Hunza. It is comparatively more developed than the surrounding regions due to a number of NGOs working there that help to spread education and economic development. Most speakers are educated up through 12th grade in Gilgit, a major city in the region of Gilgit-Baltistan a small distance from Hunza. There, the speakers are exposed to number of other regional languages such Wakhi, Shina, and Khuwar. Thus, they are exposed to a multilingual environment from early on in their lives. Most of the schools in Gilgit are what are know as “English medium” schools, where most of the curriculum is taught in written English.

The study uses semi-structured interview which were mostly 30 to 50 minutes long from 30 speakers of Burushaski. The interviews were transcribed to find recurring patterns and themes and were analysed for the discursive linguistic means which serve to construct unity, sameness, diference and origin. These include Personal reference: e.g. We, they; spatial reference: toponyms for, example, names of places, or spatial references constructed through personal pronouns (e.g.with us,with them); temporal reference: Temporal prepositions(e.g. ‘then’,‘now’) adverbs of time (e.g. ‘long ago’) (Wodak et al 1999). It was observed that how the notions of dominance, culture, and affect are constructed in discourse of ideology and it will also inquire into how these ideologies covertly expressed through stereotypes. It was seen that the Burushaski speakers demonstrate a more negative attitude towards their “National Language” than English which is seen as an international language.
