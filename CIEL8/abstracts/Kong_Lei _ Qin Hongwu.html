The Development of Manner of Speaking Markers in English and Chinese: Pragmaticalization, Grammaticalization, and Lexicalization

Lei KONG, Hongwu QIN, College of Foreign Languages, Qufu Normal University

The notions of lexicalization, grammaticalization and pragmaticalization have been standing at the center of language change research over the past decades. There are some instances of linguistic items that, at a superficial glance, can be assigned grammatical, lexical or pragmatic status, but such assignment might be problematic. For example, pragmatic markers are not easy to be given such a status, and they have aroused a lot of attention as well as controversies in linguistic literature.

Pragmatic markers, especially many phrasal markers, have been seen as lexicalized because they show some degree of fusion (Schiffrin, 1987; Fischer, 2007). However, some others argue that, rather than being lexicalized, they are grammaticalized because they have undergone many of the changes characteristic of grammaticalization (Traugott, 1995; Traugott and Dasher, 2002; Hopper and Traugott, 2003; Brinton and Traugott, 2005). There are still other scholars argue that they undergone pragmaticalization rather than grammaticalization because they have non-truth-conditional meaning and they function pragmatically (Erman and Kotsinas, 1993; Aijmer, 1997). Different views on the classification of pragmatic markers adds to the complexity of the issue. Maybe it is far too general to understand the evolution of pragmatic markers by saying it is a process of mere lexicalization, grammaticalization or pragmaticalization. In fact, the processes do not run in parallelism; pragmaticalization can be the underlying motivation for the other two processes. This assumption is the basis on which this study observes the interplay among lexicalization, grammaticalization and pragmaticalization.

To explore the possible inter-relations among the above-mentioned processes of language change, this study takes a close examination on the development of manner of speaking markers (MSM for short) by probing into the way in which English MSM honestly and Chinese MSM shuoshihua (to tell the truth) evolve into pragmatic markers. Specifically, this study attempts to address the following issues.

a. The path of change involved in the evolution of English and Chinese MSM.

b. The language universal and specific features revealed in the evolution of English and Chinese MSM.

To make it usage-based, the study is aided by empirical historical data. And the data are mainly from Google-books corpus (English) and CCL corpus (Chinese).

The observation suggests that English and Chinese MSM follow similar path in their development. Firstly, both English and Chinese MSM have their origins in clause internal compositional expressions (verb of speech + manner adv. / verb of speech + noun phrase). These expressions with verbs of speech contain a speaker perspective, thus they have a high potential to evolve into pragmatic markers and a high possibility to become independent from the proposition. When driven by the pragmatic need of emphasizing manner of speaking, these expressions first experienced a syntactic change to a more prominent position by moving to the left periphery of the sentence. This change involves hierarchical reanalysis, a formal change leading to a new structure with changed scope relations. The change of syntactic position then brought a change of the syntactic behaviour of the verbs of speech (the verbs of speech have undergone decategorization: from finite to non-finite). All these are typical features of grammaticalization and this grammaticalization is pragmatic-driven. And the grammaticalized expressions gradually become more condensed with the push of pragmatic principle of economy, evolving into lexical forms or idiomatic expressions. This is a process of lexicalization. In short, pragmatic motivation first led to grammaticalization of MSM, then their lexicalization.

Secondly, grammaticalization and lexicalization of MSM would in turn promote their pragmaticalization. For instance, change of syntactic position brought the change of semantics of the expressions, enabling them to gain illocutionary force. At the meantime, the conceptual meaning of the expressions became weaker while their communicative meaning became more prominent. With increasing frequency of use, the pragmatic function of the grammaticalized expressions became more and more fixed, and their role as pragmatic markers strengthened. Moreover, lexicalization of the expressions results in more condensed and concise forms. Being concise is an important feature of pragmatic makers. In this sense, lexicalization would also quicken the pace of pragmaticalization.

In short, diachronic data from English and Chinese show that the evolution of MSM is a complex process where Grammaticalization, pragmaticalization, and lexicalization are interwoven.

However_English MSM and Chinese MSM demonstrate different patterns of lexicalization. For English, it is the mechanism of metonymy that leads to the formal reduction of a complex phrase to a single adverb. For Chinese, the typical lexicalization pattern is the loss of semantic compositionality, yet that process does not yield a one-word marker (like an adverb in English). Considering that difference, we say English and Chinese MSM are in different stages of lexicalization, with Chinese being less lexicalized, but only idiomaticized. This shows that lexicalization of MSM is but one possible result of grammaticalization and pragmaticalization.

The lexicalization of MSM is affected by various factors, among which morphology might be the major one, that is, the pattern and the degree of lexicalization of MSM are related to the morphological features peculiar to a language. For instance, English, rich in morphological devices, is more capable of conflating various conceptual categories in lexical forms. While Chinese, a language short of morphological devices, is less capable of doing that. Therefore, behind the universal laws of lexicalization, there are typological variations concerning the pattern and degree of lexicalization.
